Mapping the connections that help democracy endure.

In recent years, democracy has been tested by rapid change, growing pressures, and complex crises. In this context, we have become more distant from the meaning of democratic concepts in everyday life: what they mean for citizens, how they protect us, and why they matter for our shared future.

This platform helps define, map, and illustrate democratic resilience in plain language. It brings together curated knowledge so that democratic resilience becomes easier to understand, discuss, teach, and use, as a practical foundation for protecting rights and freedoms, strengthening security, and supporting a prosperous democratic future.

When the connections between actors are open, diverse, trustworthy, and accountable, democracy can better absorb shocks, respond to crises, resist manipulation, and renew itself. When these connections are weakened, captured, polarised, or broken, democracy becomes more fragile. Seeing democracy as a network helps us understand where resilience comes from, where vulnerabilities appear, and where action is needed — through education, legislation, policy, institutional reform, research, civic participation, and collective action.

The lens we use

Input → Throughput → Output

We read democratic resilience as a living cycle. Citizens and their ties feed inputs into the network; structures, functions, capabilities and rules are the throughput — the mechanisms that process them; and democratic goods come out as outputs. Threats are stressors that bear on every stage. Analytical lens adapted from Scharpf (1999) and Schmidt (2013), and co-developed in “Building Capacity for Integrated Resilience Research on Ukraine and Beyond: Mixed Methods, Network Analysis and Collaborative Research Design,” co-designed and co-delivered by Dr. Inna Melnykovska and Dr. Silvia Fierăscu, at European University Institute, EUI Widening Europe Programme, Florence, Italy (27-28 April 2026).

About this project

networked-resilience.eu is a plain-language reference for the networked aspects of democratic resilience. Most democracy work focuses on individual institutions — courts, elections, parliaments, the press. This project starts from a different premise: democracies are networks of relationships between citizens, civil society, public bodies, media, researchers and responsible businesses. Their resilience depends on the quality of those connections.

The strength of a democracy is not measured by any single institution, but by the density, diversity and integrity of the ties between them.

The lens: input → throughput → output

We read networked democratic resilience as a cycle, using the input → throughput (mechanisms) → output framework adapted from democratic-legitimacy literature and co-developed in “Building Capacity for Integrated Resilience Research on Ukraine and Beyond: Mixed Methods, Network Analysis and Collaborative Research Design,” co-designed and co-delivered by Dr. Inna Melnykovska and Dr. Silvia Fierăscu, at European University Institute, EUI Widening Europe Programme, Florence, Italy (27-28 April 2026). It builds on Fritz Scharpf's distinction between input and output legitimacy and Vivien Schmidt's addition of throughput legitimacy — the quality of the mechanisms in between:

  • Input — who takes part and what flows in (actors, ties).
  • Throughput / mechanisms — how the network processes inputs (structures, functions, capabilities, rules).
  • Output — the democratic goods produced (outcomes).
  • Stressors — threats and shocks bear on every stage; resilience is keeping the cycle running under stress.

What is in the ontology

Eight branches organise the vocabulary across four layers (root → branch → sub-category → leaf), mapped onto the three phases above:

  • Actors — who is in the network
  • Ties — what connects them
  • Structures — the shapes those connections form
  • Functions — what democratic networks actually do
  • Threats — what damages the network
  • Capabilities — the resilience properties that let networks absorb, adapt and renew
  • Rules & governance — the legal and institutional scaffolding
  • Outcomes — the democratic goods produced when the network works

How definitions are written

Every entry is written in plain language first. The aim is that a curious citizen, a journalist, a public official or a civil society practitioner can read the definition and immediately know what the concept means and why it matters before going to curated sources.

How sources are used

Each concept lists one or more sources that help ground it, but the definitions are author syntheses unless otherwise stated. Sources include the V-Dem Institute, International IDEA, Freedom House, the European Commission (including the Defence of Democracy Package, EDAP, DSA, Anti-SLAPP Directive, Rule of Law Report), the Council of Europe and OSCE/ODIHR, OECD, UNDP, the Carnegie Endowment, and seminal academic work on structural holes and brokerage (Burt), weak ties (Granovetter), networked public spheres (Benkler), the network society (Castells), polycentric governance (Ostrom), the “4 R's” of resilience (Bruneau et al.), democratic legitimacy (Scharpf, Schmidt) and democratic backsliding (Levitsky & Ziblatt, Diamond).
